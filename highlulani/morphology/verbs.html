Verbs

A verb is a necessary part of any sentence, except when making copular sentences. The arguments of a verb are those nouns which accompany it in order to construct a sentence. The two main arguments are the subject and the object, and verbs can be categorised by which of these are obligatory.

Basic Verbs

The class of basic verbs is semi-closed. Basic verbs consist of one stem.

’i i to say

dissu D;z to feel

’usa us to see

janni j;N to move

kissa K;s to fight

miku M> to be red

Compound Verbs

In order to extend or specify the meaning of many verbs, verbs can be compounded much as a noun can. Compound verbs are, however, much more common than compound nouns, to make up for the relative lack of basic verbs.

“to tell a story”

“to be happy”

“to be conscious”

Quasi-Transitve Verbs

These are intransitive verbs in that they do not require a noun phrase in the object position. However, they act transitively by having a second noun phrase marked in a particular way.

Oblique and Adpositional Arguments

These are marked with an oblique case marker:

Luna kalujifiru’i mala; luna kalu’ilupagu mala.

.Wn / kWJFwi / ml , Wn / kWiWp< / ml.

Luna kalu-jifiru’i mala; luna kalu-’ilu-pagu mala.

Moon ablablative-lake reflect; moon ablablative-heart-1genfirst person genitive reflect.

“The moon is reflected in the lake; the moon is reflected in my heart.”

or an adposition:

ʔaggami niʔirri’a tihu.

.a;gM / N;i;Ra / TV.

ʔaggami ni-ʔirri’a tihu.

Police_officer in-Irìa abide.

“The police officer lives in Irìa.”

Symmetric Relations

The participants of these verbs are equivalent to each other, that is, if Alice acts on Bob, then Bob acts on Alice in the same way. When the subject is Alice, say, then Bob can:

1. Be placed in the object position. Despite the structure, this is not transitive, as the verb has no passive, and pronouns use the intransitive form:

ʔallisi sisa Bubu.

.a;LS / Ss / OO.

ʔallisi sisa Bubu.

Alice contact Bob.

“Alice is touching Bob.”

2. Take dative marking:

ʔallisi kuBubu ju’i.

.a;LS / >OO / Yi.

ʔallisi ku-Bubu ju’i.

Alice datdative-Bob link.

“Alice is married to Bob.”

3. Be conjoined with the subject:

ʔallisi Bubuta hulla.

.a;LS / OOt / V;l.

ʔallisi Bubu-ta hulla.

Alice Bob-and copulate.

“Alice and Bob are having sex.”

The basic meaning of these sentences is not changed when swapping the particpants or using the alternate structures.

sisa Ss to touch

ju’i Yi to join

hulla V;l to copulate

Action Verbs

The objects of these verbs are activities. These can take the form of pure nouns:

Hisutuba tara sinna.

.Hzeb / tr / S;n.

hisutuba tara sinna.

Babysitter begin story.

“The babysitter started the story.”

They can also take gerunds:

Kipu nu dasiruqa hafi.

.Ko / I / dSwq / hF.

Kipu nu dasi-ru-qa hafi.

Ox stop eat-gergerund-gengenitive grass.

“The cow stopped its eating of the grass.”

Perceptive Verbs

These verbs deal with the subject’s ability to perceive stimuli. They are ambitransitive, that is, they can be used with or without an argument in the object position.

When used purely intransitively, with no direct or dative object, they refer to a general ability to use that sense:

Guli ruku ’usa.

.<L / w> / us.

Guli ruku ’usa.

Blind_person fut;gnofuture gnomic see.

“The blind person can now see.”

When used with a direct object, this denotes a conscious effort at perception:

Ju’ipu’a ’usa bumaki.

.Yioa / us / OmK.

Ju’ipu’a ’usa bumaki.

Assembly see screen.

“The audience watched the screen.”

When used with a dative object, this instead lacks that effort:

Mihu kulasidaxi ju’isataduru ’usa.

.MV / >lSdX / YistEw / us.

Mihu ku-lasida-xi ju’isatadu-ru ’usa.

3ani;intanimate third person intransitive datdative-secret-seem conversation-gergerund see.

“She saw the secret meeting.”
